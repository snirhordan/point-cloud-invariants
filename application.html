Application: \(S_n\) on symmetric matrices

Now specialize to \(G=S_n\) acting on \(V_n=S^2(\R^n)\), symmetric \(n\times n\) matrices ŌĆö weighted graphs (with self-loops) under vertex relabeling. Separating invariants here are complete graph invariants. We produce a group element whose determinant carries a huge cyclotomic factor, and the machine from the previous page does the rest.

1. The representation

A basis of \(V_n=S^2(\R^n)\) is \(f_{ij}=e_{ij}+e_{ji}\) for \(1\le i\le j\le n\) (with \(f_{ii}=e_{ii}\)); \(\dim V_n=\binom{n+1}{2}\). The group \(S_n\) acts by conjugation with permutation matrices, \(X\mapsto P_\sigma X P_\sigma^{-1}\), which on the basis is simply \[ \sigma\cdot f_{ij}=f_{\sigma(i)\,\sigma(j)} . \] So \(\rho(\sigma)\) is a permutation matrix: it permutes the basis, indexed by unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) (edges, plus loops \(\{i,i\}\)). Vertices are the \(n\) points; \(S_n\) is relabeling; orbits are isomorphism classes of weighted graphs.

Lemma ŌĆö determinant of a permutation action If \(\rho(\sigma)\) permutes a basis with orbits \(O_1,\dots,O_m\) of sizes \(|O_k|\), then \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))=\prod_{k=1}^{m}\big(1-t^{|O_k|}\big)\).
Proof. In the basis grouped by orbits, \(\rho(\sigma)\) is block-diagonal, one block per orbit, each block an \(\ell\times\ell\) cyclic permutation matrix \(C_\ell\) (\(\ell=|O_k|\)). Expanding along the first row, \(\det(I-tC_\ell)=1-t^\ell\). Multiply the blocks.
Example ŌĆö \(\sigma=(1\,2)(3\,4\,5)\) in \(S_5\) on \(V_5\) Here \(\dim V_5=15\), basis \(f_{ij}\), \(1\le i\le j\le5\). The \(\langle\sigma\rangle\)-orbits:
• loops: \(f_{11}\to f_{22}\to f_{11}\) (size 2) and \(f_{33}\to f_{44}\to f_{55}\to f_{33}\) (size 3);
• the edge inside the 2-cycle: \(f_{12}\) is fixed ŌĆö \(\sigma\) swaps its endpoints, \(\{1,2\}\to\{2,1\}\) (size 1);
• edges inside the 3-cycle: \(f_{34}\to f_{45}\to f_{35}\to f_{34}\) (size 3);
• all \(2\cdot3=6\) cross edges in a single orbit of size \(\lcm(2,3)=6\): \(f_{13}\to f_{24}\to f_{15}\to f_{23}\to f_{14}\to f_{25}\to f_{13}\).
Sizes \(2+3+1+3+6=15\) Ō£ō. One block explicitly: on \(\{f_{33},f_{44},f_{55}\}\), \[ I-tC_3=\begin{pmatrix}1&-t&0\\0&1&-t\\-t&0&1\end{pmatrix},\qquad\det=1-t^3, \] and altogether \[ \det\big(I-t\rho(\sigma)\big)=(1-t)\,(1-t^2)\,(1-t^3)^2\,(1-t^6). \] The cross-orbit factor \(1-t^6\) is the engine of the theorem below in miniature: with \(p=2\), \(q=3\) it carries \(\Phi_6(t)=t^2-t+1\), forcing some separating degree \(d_i\) divisible by \(6=pq\) (┬¦4). Note also why the note's \(f_{1,p}\) slips: \(f_{12}\) is a within-cycle pair ŌĆö here even a fixed point ŌĆö not a size-\(pq\) orbit.

2. Cycle-type determinant formula

So computing \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))\) is a matter of counting orbit sizes of \(\langle\sigma\rangle\) on unordered pairs. Let \(\sigma\) have cycle type \((c_1,\dots,c_k)\). The one count we actually need is the cross-cycle one, which we prove; the full formula is recorded after.

Lemma ŌĆö cross-cycle orbits Let \(C_a,C_b\) be two distinct cycles of \(\sigma\), of lengths \(a,b\). The unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) with \(i\in C_a\), \(j\in C_b\) form exactly \(\gcd(a,b)\) orbits of \(\langle\sigma\rangle\), each of size \(\lcm(a,b)\). They contribute the factor \(\big(1-t^{\lcm(a,b)}\big)^{\gcd(a,b)}\).
Proof. There are \(ab\) such pairs. The orbit of \(\{i,j\}\) has size the least \(m>0\) with \(\{\sigma^m i,\sigma^m j\}=\{i,j\}\). Since \(i,j\) lie in different cycles, \(\sigma^m\) cannot swap them, so we need \(\sigma^m i=i\) and \(\sigma^m j=j\), i.e. \(a\mid m\) and \(b\mid m\); the least such \(m\) is \(\lcm(a,b)\). Every orbit thus has size \(\lcm(a,b)\), so their number is \(ab/\lcm(a,b)=\gcd(a,b)\).
Full formula (for reference). Collecting cross-cycle, within-cycle, and loop contributions gives \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))=\prod\big(1-t^{\lcm(c_p,c_q)}\big)^{\gcd(c_p,c_q)}\times(\text{within-cycle terms})\). Only the cross-cycle factors matter below: within-cycle and loop orbits have size \(\le\max_i c_i\le n\), so they can never supply a factor of order \(\sim n^2\).

3. The construction

Fix \(\varepsilon>0\). By the prime number theorem the interval \(\big((\tfrac12-\varepsilon)n,\ \tfrac12 n\big)\) contains \(\sim\varepsilon n/\ln n\to\infty\) primes, so for all large \(n\) it holds two distinct primes \(p,q\) with \((\tfrac12-\varepsilon)n<p,q<\tfrac12 n\). Then \(p+q<n\), so we may define \[ \sigma=(1\ 2\ \cdots\ p)\,(p{+}1\ p{+}2\ \cdots\ p{+}q)\ \in S_n, \] a product of a \(p\)-cycle on \(\{1,\dots,p\}\) and a \(q\)-cycle on \(\{p{+}1,\dots,p{+}q\}\) (the remaining points fixed).

Clarification (source slip #1). Harm's note takes the basis element \(f_{1,p}\), but both \(1\) and \(p\) lie in the \(p\)-cycle, giving a within-cycle orbit ŌĆö not size \(pq\). The intended element is a cross edge, e.g. \(f_{1,\,p+1}\): endpoint \(1\) in the \(p\)-cycle, endpoint \(p{+}1\) in the \(q\)-cycle. With that fix the argument goes through verbatim.

Apply the cross-cycle Lemma with \(a=p\), \(b=q\), \(\gcd(p,q)=1\): the orbit of the cross edge \(f_{1,p+1}\) has size \(\lcm(p,q)=pq\). (Directly: \(\sigma^k\) sends it to \(\{\sigma^k 1,\sigma^k(p{+}1)\}\), and by CRT the pair (position mod \(p\), position mod \(q\)) runs through all \(pq\) values.) By the permutation-determinant Lemma, \[ \big(1-t^{pq}\big)\ \Big|\ \det\!\big(I-t\rho(\sigma)\big). \]

4. The lower bound

Main Theorem \(\displaystyle \liminf_{n\to\infty}\frac{\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})}{n^2}\ \ge\ \frac14.\)
Proof. Let \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) be homogeneous separating invariants of degrees \(d_1,\dots,d_r\); we bound \(\max_i d_i\). By the separating Corollary (Theorems ┬¦3), \(p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\). Since \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))\mid p(t)\) (it is one of the lcm's terms) and \((1-t^{pq})\mid\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))\) (┬¦3), we get \[ (1-t^{pq})\ \Big|\ \prod_{i=1}^{r}(1-t^{d_i}). \] Hence \(\Phi_{pq}\mid(1-t^{pq})\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\). As \(\Phi_{pq}\) is irreducible (Theorems ┬¦4), it divides a single factor \(1-t^{d_i}\), which forces \[ d_i\ \ge\ \deg\Phi_{pq}\ =\ (p-1)(q-1). \] Therefore \(\max_i d_i\ge(p-1)(q-1)\), i.e. \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})\ge(p-1)(q-1)\). With \(p,q>(\tfrac12-\varepsilon)n\), \[ \frac{\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})}{n^2}\ \ge\ \frac{(p-1)(q-1)}{n^2}\ \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}\ \Big(\tfrac12-\varepsilon\Big)^2 . \] Since \(\varepsilon>0\) was arbitrary, the \(\liminf\) is \(\ge 1/4\).
Clarification (source slip #2). Harm's "cyclic polynomial \(\phi_{pq}\) of degree \((p-1)(q-1)\)" is the cyclotomic polynomial \(\Phi_{pq}\); its degree is \(\varphi(pq)=(p-1)(q-1)\), and its irreducibility over \(\Z\) is what makes it divide a single \(1-t^{d_i}\).
Directed variant. On all matrices \(\R^{n\times n}\) (ordered pairs, i.e. directed weighted graphs), the same \(\sigma\) has a cross-arc orbit of size \(pq\) as well, so the identical bound \(\liminf\ge 1/4\) holds there too.
What was proved. Any complete/separating set of polynomial invariants of \(n\)-vertex weighted graphs contains an invariant of degree at least \((\tfrac12-o(1))^2 n^2\sim n^2/4\). Low-degree complete invariants of graphs simply do not exist. The Significance page draws out the consequences.

5. An upper bound: complete invariants in degree \(O(n^4)\)

The lower bound says \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\gtrsim n^2/4\). How large must it be? Harm's note closes with a ceiling: a completely explicit construction separating any two orbits with an invariant of degree \(O(n^4)\). For graphs this sandwiches the separating degree, \[ \big(\tfrac12-o(1)\big)^2 n^2\ \le\ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})\ \le\ \tfrac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\ \sim\ \tfrac12 n^4 . \]

Upper bound Write \(N=\dim V_n=\binom{n+1}{2}=\tfrac{n^2+n}{2}\). The invariant polynomials of degree \(\le N(2N-1)=\tfrac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\) separate the \(S_n\)-orbits on \(V_n\).

The idea is an orbit sum of a spike. Build a polynomial that reads \(1\) on the entries of \(A\) and \(0\) on any entry value \(A\) does not have; average it over \(S_n\) to make it invariant. The result "fires" on \(A\)'s orbit and vanishes on \(B\)'s. Unlike the lower-bound argument, the same construction works for every pair, so no group representation theory is needed here ŌĆö just Lagrange interpolation and symmetrization.

Proof. Fix \(A,B\in V_n\) in distinct orbits; we exhibit one invariant of degree \(\le N(2N-1)\) separating them. A symmetric matrix has \(N\) independent entries \(X_{ij}\) (\(i\le j\)). Consider the finite set of reals \[ \mathcal V=\{A_{kl}:k\le l\}\cup\{B_{kl}:k\le l\},\qquad |\mathcal V|\le 2N . \] Spikes. For each pair \(i\le j\), let \(\ell_{ij}\) be the univariate (Lagrange) polynomial with \[ \ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1,\qquad \ell_{ij}(v)=0\ \text{ for every } v\in\mathcal V\setminus\{A_{ij}\}. \] It prescribes values at \(\le 2N\) distinct points, so \(\deg\ell_{ij}\le 2N-1\). (Here \(\mathcal V\setminus\{A_{ij}\}\) removes the value \(A_{ij}\); the spike is well defined even when several entries happen to coincide.)

Symmetrize. Set \[ P(X)=\sum_{\tau\in S_n}\ \prod_{i\le j}\ell_{ij}\big(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\big). \] The inner product runs over the \(N\) unordered pairs. Replacing \(X\) by \(\sigma\cdot X\) sends \(X_{ab}\mapsto X_{\sigma^{-1}(a),\sigma^{-1}(b)}\), so the \(\tau\)-term becomes the \((\sigma^{-1}\tau)\)-term; reindexing \(\tau\mapsto\sigma\tau\) leaves the sum unchanged. Hence \(P\in K[V_n]^{S_n}\).

It separates. Every factor \(\ell_{ij}(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)})\), evaluated at an entry of \(A\) or of \(B\), lands on a point of \(\mathcal V\), so it equals \(0\) or \(1\) ŌĆö and it is \(1\) exactly when \(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}=A_{ij}\) as values. Thus each product is \(0\) or \(1\), and equals \(1\) iff \(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}=A_{ij}\) for all \(i\le j\), i.e. iff \(\tau^{-1}\!\cdot X=A\). Counting such \(\tau\): \[ P(A)=\#\{\tau:\tau^{-1}\!\cdot A=A\}=|\mathrm{Aut}(A)|\ \ge\ 1,\qquad P(B)=\#\{\tau:\tau^{-1}\!\cdot B=A\}=0, \] the second because no \(\tau\) carries \(B\) into \(A\)'s orbit. So \(P(A)\neq P(B)\): \(P\) separates them.

Degree. For each \(\tau\), the product is a polynomial in \(N\) distinct coordinates, each occurring to degree \(\le 2N-1\); its total degree is therefore \(\le N(2N-1)\), and summing over \(\tau\) preserves this. Hence \(\deg P\le N(2N-1)=\tfrac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\).
Error check. The construction is correct as stated: for each pair of distinct orbits it yields an invariant of degree \(\le\tfrac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\) that separates them, so \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le\tfrac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\). Three points are worth making explicit ŌĆö none breaks the proof:
  1. Coincident entries. \(\mathcal V\) is a set of values. If \(A_{ij}\) equals some \(B_{kl}\), the spike \(\ell_{ij}\) is still well defined (value \(1\) at that shared point), and \(P(B)=0\) survives because a product hits \(1\) only under a full entrywise match \(\tau^{-1}B=A\), which distinct orbits forbid.
  2. Why zero out all of \(\mathcal V\), not just \(B\)'s values. Forcing \(\ell_{ij}\) to vanish at the other \(A\)-values too makes every product lie in \(\{0,1\}\); the non-identity terms are then \(\ge 0\) and cannot cancel the identity term. That is exactly what secures Harm's "\(P(A)\ge1\)" ŌĆö in fact \(P(A)=|\mathrm{Aut}(A)|\). Interpolating on only \(N+1\) points would shave the degree but lose this nonnegativity guarantee.
  3. Looseness. The per-factor bound \(2N-1\) and the product-of-\(N\) bound are both generous, and the degree is an upper bound on one particular separator, not the minimum. So \(O(n^4)\) is an honest ceiling, far above the \(\sim n^2/4\) floor.
The sandwich. For \(n\)-vertex weighted graphs, \[ \tfrac14\,n^2\ \lesssim\ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})\ \lesssim\ \tfrac12\,n^4 . \] Complete polynomial graph invariants exist in degree \(O(n^4)\) and require degree \(\Omega(n^2)\). Closing the exponent gap between \(2\) and \(4\) is open.

6. A second example: \(S_n\) on \(\R^n\) (an exact case)

The same machine, on a smaller representation, pins down \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) exactly ŌĆö a reassuring check, and the natural "unordered points on a line" model. Let \(S_n\) act on \(\R^n\) by permuting coordinates, \(\sigma\cdot(x_1,\dots,x_n)=(x_{\sigma^{-1}(1)},\dots,x_{\sigma^{-1}(n)})\) ŌĆö the permutation (defining) representation. Orbits are multisets of \(n\) reals; the invariants are the symmetric polynomials.

Exact bound \(\displaystyle \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\big(K[\R^n]^{S_n}\big)=n\quad\text{for every }n\ge1.\)
Upper bound \(\le n\). The elementary symmetric polynomials \(e_1,\dots,e_n\), of degrees \(1,\dots,n\), are separating: two vectors share an \(S_n\)-orbit iff they have the same multiset of coordinates iff the same characteristic polynomial \(\prod_i(x-x_i)\), i.e. the same \(e_1,\dots,e_n\). Their maximal degree is \(n\), so \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le n\).
Lower bound \(\ge n\). Let \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) be separating invariants of degrees \(d_1,\dots,d_r\). The invariants vanish simultaneously only at \(0\) (if \(e_1=\cdots=e_n=0\) then \(\prod_i(x-x_i)=x^n\), forcing \(x=0\)), so by Lemma 2 the separating set has zero set \(\{0\}\) and the divisibility Corollary (Theorems ┬¦3) gives \(p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\). Take \(\sigma\) an \(n\)-cycle: on \(\R^n\) it permutes the basis in a single orbit of size \(n\), so \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))=1-t^n\). Hence \(\Phi_n\mid(1-t^n)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\); as \(\Phi_n\) is irreducible it divides a single factor \(1-t^{d_i}\), which means \(n\mid d_i\), hence \(d_i\ge n\). So \(\max_i d_i\ge n\).

The two bounds meet: \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[\R^n]^{S_n})=n\), attained exactly by the elementary symmetric polynomials.

Why exact here, only a \(\liminf\) for graphs. In both cases a single group element supplies a cyclotomic factor. For graphs it was \(1-t^{pq}\) with \(pq\sim n^2/4\), but "\(\Phi_{pq}\mid 1-t^{d_i}\)" only forces \(d_i\ge(p-1)(q-1)\), and \(p,q\) are merely near \(n/2\) ŌĆö hence a limit. Here the \(n\)-cycle gives \(1-t^n\) directly, and "\(\Phi_n\mid 1-t^{d_i}\)" gives the clean divisibility \(n\mid d_i\), so \(d_i\ge n\) on the nose. The polynomial cost of a complete descriptor of \(n\) unordered points on the line is exactly \(n\) ŌĆö linear, against quadratic for graphs.
The standard \((n-1)\)-dimensional representation. On the irreducible \(W=\{x\in\R^n:\sum_i x_i=0\}\) the story is identical. An \(n\)-cycle acts on \(W\) with \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma)|_W)=\dfrac{1-t^n}{1-t}=1+t+\cdots+t^{n-1}\), still divisible by \(\Phi_n\); and \(K[W]^{S_n}=\R[e_2,\dots,e_n]\) is separating with top degree \(n\). So \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[W]^{S_n})=n\) as well ŌĆö the trivial summand carried none of the cost.
