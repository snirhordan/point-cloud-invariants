Point clouds: \(O(d)\times S_n\) on \(\R^{d\times n}\)

The note's §3 attacks the true point-cloud setting: \(n\) points in \(\R^d\) up to rotation/reflection and relabeling. The Gram-map reduction to the graph setting is clean and correct. The claimed \(O(d^2n^2)\) separation degree, however, rests on a proof with a fatal gap; what the method honestly yields is a d-independent \(\sim n^4/4\) bound.

1. Setting

Definition For \(d\le n\), let \(O(d)\times S_n\) act on \(X\in\R^{d\times n}\) (columns = \(n\) points in \(\R^d\)) by \((R,P)\cdot X=RXP^T\). The separation degree \(D(n,d)\) is the least \(D\) such that for every \(X,Y\) in different orbits some invariant polynomial of degree \(\le D\) takes different values — i.e. \(D(n,d)=\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) of this invariant ring.

Translations. Physical point clouds also quotient by translation; center the configuration first (\(\sum_i x_i=0\)) and the group above is what remains. This is the \(d=3\) symmetry group of molecular ML.

2. The Gram reduction (correct)

Define \(\Phi:\R^{d\times n}\to V_n=S^2(\R^n)\), \(\Phi(X)=X^TX\). Its image is the cone \(\mathcal G_d=\{G\in V_n: G\succeq0,\ \rank G\le d\}\), of dimension \(N_d=dn-\binom d2\).

Proposition 1 — orbit criterion \(\Phi(X)=\Phi(Y)\iff Y=RX\) for some \(R\in O(d)\).
Proof. (\(\Leftarrow\)) clear. (\(\Rightarrow\)) Equal Gram matrices mean the columns have identical pairwise inner products, so \(x_i\mapsto y_i\) extends to a linear isometry \(\operatorname{span}(X)\to\operatorname{span}(Y)\); extend it to \(R\in O(d)\) by mapping an orthonormal complement to an orthonormal complement. Then \(Y=RX\).
Proposition 2 — equivariance \(\Phi\big((R,P)\cdot X\big)=(RXP^T)^T(RXP^T)=P\,X^TX\,P^T=P\,\Phi(X)\,P^T\): \(\Phi\) is \(O(d)\)-invariant and \(S_n\)-equivariant.
Corollary — reduction \(X,Y\) lie in the same \(O(d)\times S_n\)-orbit \(\iff\) \(\Phi(X),\Phi(Y)\) lie in the same \(S_n\)-orbit in \(\mathcal G_d\subset V_n\).
Proof. If \(Y=RXP^T\) then \(\Phi(Y)=P\Phi(X)P^T\) (Prop. 2). Conversely if \(\Phi(Y)=P\Phi(X)P^T=\Phi(XP^T)\), Prop. 1 gives \(Y=R\,(XP^T)\).
Losslessness (Weyl's First Fundamental Theorem for \(O(d)\), classical import). Every \(O(d)\)-invariant polynomial of \(X\) is a polynomial in the Gram entries \((X^TX)_{ij}\). Hence every \(O(d)\times S_n\)-invariant is \(F\circ\Phi\) with \(F\) an \(S_n\)-invariant polynomial function on \(\mathcal G_d\), and \(\deg(F\circ\Phi)=2\deg F\) for \(F\) homogeneous. So \[ D(n,d)\ =\ 2\,\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\big(K[\mathcal G_d]^{S_n}\big): \] the point-cloud problem is the graph problem restricted to the rank-\(\le d\) PSD cone — in both directions, upper and lower.

3. The Lagrange lemma (statement true; the note's proof repaired)

Lemma — separation for permutation actions Let a finite group \(G\) act on \(\R^N\) by permuting coordinates, and let \(A,B\in\R^N\) lie in different \(G\)-orbits. Then there is a \(G\)-invariant polynomial \(Q\) of degree \(\le N(2N-1)\) with \(Q(A)\neq Q(B)\).
Proof (corrected). For each coordinate \(k\) let \(\Lambda_k=\{\sigma(A)_k:\sigma\in G\}\cup\{\sigma(B)_k:\sigma\in G\}\). Since \(G\) permutes coordinates, \(\sigma(A)_k\) is one of the \(N\) coordinate values of \(A\) (and likewise for \(B\)), so \(|\Lambda_k|\le 2N\). Let \(\ell_k\) be the Lagrange polynomial with \(\ell_k(A_k)=1\), vanishing on \(\Lambda_k\setminus\{A_k\}\); \(\deg\ell_k\le 2N-1\). Set \(Q(M)=\sum_{\sigma\in G}\prod_{k=1}^N\ell_k(\sigma(M)_k)\); it is \(G\)-invariant (reindexing). At \(M=A\): every \(\sigma(A)_k\in\Lambda_k\), so each factor is \(0\) or \(1\); the identity term is \(1\); hence \(Q(A)\ge1\). At \(M=B\): for each \(\sigma\) there is \(k\) with \(\sigma(B)_k\neq A_k\), and \(\sigma(B)_k\in\Lambda_k\), so \(\ell_k\) vanishes there and the term is \(0\); hence \(Q(B)=0\). Finally \(\deg Q\le N(2N-1)\).
Step-by-step version — every move justified, and where the note's proof breaks

Step 0 — the nodes are few, and why. The action is \((\sigma\cdot M)_k=M_{\sigma^{-1}(k)}\): coordinate \(k\) of a permuted vector is some original coordinate of that vector. Hence \(\Lambda_k=\{\sigma(A)_k\}\cup\{\sigma(B)_k\}\subseteq\{A_1,\dots,A_N\}\cup\{B_1,\dots,B_N\}\), so \(|\Lambda_k|\le2N\) — counted by values, not by orbit points. This is the correct version of the note's "\(|\Lambda_k|\le1+|G\cdot B|\le2N\)": the orbit \(G\cdot B\) can have up to \(|G|\) elements (\(n!\) in the application), but its \(k\)-th coordinates take at most \(N\) values (finding #4(i)).

Step 1 — \(\ell_k\) on nodes. As in the graph proof: \(\ell_k(A_k)=1\), \(\ell_k=0\) on \(\Lambda_k\setminus\{A_k\}\), \(\deg\ell_k\le|\Lambda_k|-1\le2N-1\); restricted to \(\Lambda_k\) it is the indicator of the value \(A_k\); off \(\Lambda_k\) it is arbitrary — so evaluate only at nodes.

Step 2 — invariance. \(Q(g\cdot M)=\sum_\sigma\prod_k\ell_k((\sigma g)(M)_k)\); reindex \(\sigma'=\sigma g\), a bijection of \(G\).

Step 3 — \(Q(A)\ge1\), and the note's gap. (1) Every factor is a node evaluation: \(\sigma(A)_k=A_{\sigma^{-1}(k)}\in\Lambda_k\) — because we put the orbit values of \(A\) into \(\Lambda_k\). (2) Each factor is \(0\) or \(1\) (Step 1); (3) each \(\sigma\)-term is in \(\{0,1\}\); (4) the identity term is \(\prod_k\ell_k(A_k)=1\) and all terms are \(\ge0\), so \(Q(A)\ge1\).
Where the note breaks (finding #4(ii)): with the note's \(\Lambda_k=\{A_k\}\cup\{\sigma(B)_k\}\), move (1) fails — for \(\sigma\neq\mathrm{id}\) the argument \(A_{\sigma^{-1}(k)}\) is generally not a node, so \(\ell_k\) there is an arbitrary real (possibly negative or huge), the terms are not in \([0,1]\), and cancellation down to \(Q(A)=0=Q(B)\) cannot be excluded.

Step 4 — \(Q(B)=0\). Fix \(\sigma\). If \(\sigma(B)_k=A_k\) for all \(k\), then \(\sigma(B)=A\) — same orbit, contradiction; so a witness \(k\) has \(\sigma(B)_k\neq A_k\). That argument is in \(\Lambda_k\) by construction, and being a node different in value from \(A_k\) it is a root of \(\ell_k\): the factor, hence the \(\sigma\)-term, is \(0\). All terms vanish.

Step 5 — degree, and the downstream failure. \(N\) factors of degree \(\le2N-1\) give \(\deg Q\le N(2N-1)\) — exactly the stated bound, recovered with the enlarged node set. But note what the lemma requires: \(G\) must act by coordinate permutations. In the note's application the coordinates are Cholesky parameters of the Gram matrix, on which \(S_n\) does not act by permutations — that is the separate, fatal gap of the claimed theorem (finding #5, §4 below), independent of the repair made here.

Errors in the note's proof (finding #4 — statement survives). The note takes \(\Lambda_k=\{A_k\}\cup\{\sigma(B)_k\}\) and justifies \(|\Lambda_k|\le 2N\) by "\(\le 1+|G\cdot B|\le 2N\)". Two problems. (i) Wrong count: the orbit cardinality \(|G\cdot B|\) can be as large as \(|G|\) — in the intended application \(|S_n|=n!\), vastly exceeding \(2N\). The true reason the node count is small is the one above: \(\sigma(B)_k\) ranges over at most the \(N\) coordinate values of \(B\). (ii) Broken positivity: with that smaller \(\Lambda_k\), at \(M=A\) the factors \(\ell_k(\sigma(A)_k)\) for \(\sigma\neq\mathrm{id}\) are evaluations off the node set — "values in \([0,1]\)" is unjustified; they can be arbitrary reals, and \(Q(A)\ge1\) fails (terms could cancel to \(Q(A)=0=Q(B)\)). The fix — include the orbit values of \(A\) in \(\Lambda_k\), exactly as in the correct §2 template — restores the argument and recovers precisely the stated degree \(N(2N-1)\).

4. The claimed theorem — and why its proof fails

Claim — note Thm (unproven) For \(2\le d\le n\): \(D(n,d)\le 2N_d(2N_d-1)\), where \(N_d=dn-\binom d2\) — i.e. \(O(d^2n^2)\).

The note's proof: coordinatize (a dense open part of) \(\mathcal G_d\) by the \(N_d\) free entries of the Cholesky factor, apply the Lemma with \(N=N_d\), and pull back through \(\Phi\) (each Gram entry has degree 2 in \(X\), whence the factor 2).

Fatal gaps (finding #5).
(a) Wrong group action. \(S_n\) permutes the entries of \(G\mapsto PGP^T\), but the induced maps on Cholesky coordinates are not coordinate permutations: permuting rows/columns destroys the trapezoidal form, and re-factoring recombines the coordinates nonlinearly. The Lemma's hypothesis ("\(G\) acts by coordinate permutations") fails, so the constructed \(Q\) — invariant under permutations of the \(c_k\), a different group — has no reason to be \(S_n\)-invariant on \(\mathcal G_d\).
(b) Not a polynomial. Cholesky coordinates involve square roots (\(c_{11}=\sqrt{G_{11}}\), …): they are algebraic, not polynomial, in the \(G_{ij}\). So \(p(X)=Q(c(X^TX))\) is not a polynomial in \(X\), and the degree accounting "\(2\cdot N_d(2N_d-1)\)" — which silently treats \(c_k\) as degree-1 in \(G\) — is not meaningful.
(c) Chart issues. The trapezoidal Cholesky form needs the leading \(d\times d\) principal minor nonsingular (and \(\rank=d\)); the asserted "dense open subset containing \(A\) and \(B\)" need not contain them.
Verdict: \(D(n,d)=O(d^2n^2)\) is a plausible open target, not a theorem of this note. See Audit, direction D2 for repair strategies.

5. What the method does prove

Theorem — salvaged upper bound For all \(n\ge3\) and every \(d\ge1\), \[ D(n,d)\ \le\ 2\,\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\big(K[V_n]^{S_n}\big)\ \le\ \frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{4}\ \sim\ \frac{n^4}{4}. \]
Proof. Let \(X,Y\) be in different \(O(d)\times S_n\)-orbits. By the reduction Corollary, \(A=\Phi(X)\) and \(B=\Phi(Y)\) lie in different \(S_n\)-orbits in \(V_n\). Take a homogeneous separating family on \(V_n\) of degree \(\le\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\) (Upper Bound §2); some member \(f\) has \(f(A)\neq f(B)\). Then \(p:=f\circ\Phi\) is \(O(d)\times S_n\)-invariant (Prop. 2), separates \(X,Y\), and \(\deg p=2\deg f\).

Update — a stronger repair is now proved. The first-\(d\)-rows theorem exploits the rank-\(\le d\) structure correctly (via the Gram completion identity instead of Cholesky) and improves this to \(D(n,d)\le 2N_d(n^2+n-1)\sim 2dn^3\), validated in exact arithmetic.

Notes. The bound is uniform in \(d\) — the rank-\(\le d\) structure is not exploited; that is exactly what the broken Cholesky argument was trying to do. No lower bound on \(D(n,d)\) is currently known for \(d<n\): the universal-denominator machinery needs a linear action on all of \(V\), and on the cone \(\mathcal G_d\) it does not apply as-is (Audit, D3).

6. Gram = distances

For centered configurations, \(G_{ij}=\langle x_i,x_j\rangle=\tfrac12(\|x_i\|^2+\|x_j\|^2-\|x_i-x_j\|^2)\) with the norms recoverable from the distance matrix. So the reduction identifies point-cloud completeness with relabeling-invariant completeness of the distance/Gram matrix — the graph problem — and the degree bounds here quantify its polynomial cost. See Significance.
