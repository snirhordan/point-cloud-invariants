Significance

The note now tells a two-sided story: completeness has a degree cost that is at least quadratic and at most quartic in the number of points — with the point-cloud setting reduced exactly to the graph setting via the Gram map.

1. Separating invariants = complete invariant features

A feature map \(\Phi:V\to\R^m\) is invariant and complete (separating) when \(\Phi(u)=\Phi(v)\iff u,v\) share a \(G\)-orbit. The components of a polynomial such \(\Phi\) are a separating set of invariants, so \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) is the intrinsic polynomial cost of a complete invariant descriptor: lower bounds are impossibility results, upper bounds are existence guarantees.

2. Graphs: the cost is between quadratic and quartic

BoundMechanism
Impossibility \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\ge(\tfrac14-o(1))\,n^2\) a group element with a primitive \(pq\)-th root of unity forces a degree via \(\Phi_{pq}\)
Existence \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\sim\frac{n^4}{8}\) Göbel's generation bound for permutation actions (corrected constant)

Read together: no complete polynomial descriptor of \(n\)-vertex weighted graphs can stay below degree \(\sim n^2/4\), yet complete descriptors do exist at degree \(\sim n^4/8\) — completeness is expensive but finite. The truth lies in the \(n^2\)–\(n^4\) window; the lower-bound technique is provably exhausted at \(n^2/4\) and the upper bound measures generation rather than separation, so both sides have slack (Audit D1).

3. Point clouds: the graph problem in disguise

For \(n\) points in \(\R^d\) modulo \(O(d)\times S_n\), the Gram map plus Weyl's first fundamental theorem make the reduction exact: \(D(n,d)=2\,\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[\mathcal G_d]^{S_n})\) on the rank-\(\le d\) PSD cone. Proved consequence (any \(d\), in particular molecules at \(d=3\)): \[ D(n,3)\ \le\ \frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{4}\ \sim\ \frac{n^4}{4}. \] The note's claimed improvement to \(O(d^2n^2)\) — which would be dramatic for fixed \(d=3\) — is open: its proof breaks at the Cholesky step (Point Clouds §4). No lower bound for \(D(n,d)\), \(d<n\), is known.

4. Consequences for the MACE universality program

Degree is a two-sided resource. (i) Ceiling: a complete invariant readout for \(n\)-point clouds exists at polynomial degree \(\sim n^4/4\) — a concrete, finite target for the expansion order of ACE/MACE-type feature hierarchies; a repaired \(O(d^2n^2)\) bound would lower the target to \(O(n^2)\). (ii) Floor: in the graph/Gram encoding, any architecture whose invariant readout is a polynomial of degree \(D=o(n^2)\) cannot separate all inputs — bounded-degree completeness is impossible as \(n\) grows.

Three honest caveats. (a) The floor is proved for the finite group \(S_n\) on all of \(V_n\); it transfers to point clouds only through the (open) lower-bound question on \(\mathcal G_d\). (b) The \(q\)-separated escape hatch: the lower-bound machinery needs separation on all configurations (it uses the orbit \(\{0\}\)); universality restricted to compact, collision-free, \(q\)-separated clouds is not subject to the \(\Omega(n^2)\) barrier as proved — cheaper complete invariants may exist there. (c) Degree bounds constrain the polynomial resource, not any particular architecture; they translate into correlation-order/depth requirements only through what the architecture can express per layer.

Via Gram = distances (§6), all of this is a statement about relabeling-invariant functions of the distance matrix: known counterexamples where low-order distance summaries collide are the qualitative face of these quantitative degree costs.

5. The transferable technique

Lower bounds: (1) Molien assembles the Hilbert series from \(1/\det(I-t\rho(g))\); (2) trivial zero set forces \(\det(I-t\rho(g))\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\); (3) one group element with a large-order eigenvalue yields a large irreducible cyclotomic factor. Upper bounds: (1) Lagrange-interpolate on the finitely many values of two non-equivalent inputs and symmetrize; (2) for permutation actions, Göbel bounds generation, hence separation. Both engines apply to any finite permutation-type symmetry; extending either beyond linear actions (to the rank-\(\le d\) cone, or to reductive groups via Molien–Weyl) is the open frontier (Audit D2–D3).
