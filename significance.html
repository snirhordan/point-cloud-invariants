Significance

Why a degree lower bound is the object of interest, and what it says for complete / universal invariant features and the MACE universality program.

1. Separating invariants = complete invariant features

A feature map \(\Phi:V\to\R^m\) is invariant and complete (equivalently separating, universal on orbits) when \[ \Phi(u)=\Phi(v)\iff u,v\ \text{lie in the same }G\text{-orbit}. \] The components of a polynomial such \(\Phi\) are exactly a separating set of invariants. So the least achievable maximum degree of the components is \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\): it is the intrinsic polynomial cost of building a complete invariant descriptor. This is precisely the quantity Harm bounds — a lower bound on \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) is an impossibility result for low-degree complete descriptors.

2. The message of the \(n^2/4\) bound

Restatement For weighted graphs on \(n\) vertices (\(S_n\) acting on symmetric matrices), every complete polynomial invariant descriptor must contain a component of degree \(\ge(\tfrac12-o(1))^2n^2\sim n^2/4\).

Completeness is not free: it costs polynomial degree that grows quadratically in the number of points. No clever choice of invariants escapes it — the bound is over all separating sets. The obstruction is structural: some group element rotates the space with a large-order eigenvalue (a primitive \(pq\)-th root of unity), and completeness forces the descriptor's degrees to "see" that order via the cyclotomic factor \(\Phi_{pq}\).

3. Consequences for the MACE universality program

Your goal is universality of MACE on point clouds: enough depth/width/correlation order to separate all configurations. Harm's result is the sharp version of a warning that any such program must respect.

Degree is a hard resource. If an architecture's invariant readout is a polynomial of bounded degree \(D\) (fixed independent of \(n\)), it cannot be complete once \(D<\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\sim n^2/4\). Completeness on \(n\)-point inputs demands polynomial degree \(\Omega(n^2)\).

In the ACE/MACE picture, invariant features are built from products of order-\(\nu\) tensors over message-passing layers; realizing a degree-\(D\) invariant needs correlation order and/or depth that grow with \(D\). The \(\Omega(n^2)\) degree lower bound therefore propagates into an \(n\)-dependent lower bound on the correlation order / depth required for completeness — the price is paid in the very knobs your program tunes.

Two honest caveats. (i) The theorem is for the finite group \(S_n\) on symmetric matrices (graphs), not literally the continuous \(O(3)\times S_n\) point-cloud group; it is a rigorous, clean instance of the general principle "completeness \(\Rightarrow\) high degree," and the Molien / universal-denominator + cyclotomic-factor technique carries over to other finite-group settings. (ii) It bounds the maximum degree in a separating set, i.e. the polynomial degree of a complete descriptor — it does not by itself dictate a particular architecture, only the resource any complete one must spend.

The technique, transferable. The reusable engine is: (1) Molien assembles the Hilbert series from \(1/\det(I-t\rho(g))\); (2) trivial zero set (from separation) forces \(\det(I-t\rho(g))\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\); (3) find a \(g\) whose determinant carries a high-order irreducible cyclotomic factor. Any symmetry group with an element of large-order eigenvalue relative to the ambient dimension yields a degree lower bound this way.
