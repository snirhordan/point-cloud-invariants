Background

Everything needed to state the note's theorems, assuming only representation theory, group theory, and (multi)linear algebra. Proved here: Molien's formula and Hilbert's finiteness lemma (feeding the lower bound), plus \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le\beta\) and the statement of Göbel's bound (feeding the upper bounds).

1. The invariant ring and its Hilbert series

Let \(G\) be a finite group and \(V\cong K^n\) a representation over a field \(K\) of characteristic \(0\), with \(\rho(g)\in GL(V)\) the action matrices. Polynomial functions on \(V\) form the graded ring \[ K[V]=\Sym(V^*)=\bigoplus_{d\ge 0}K[V]_d,\qquad K[V]_d=\Sym^d(V^*), \] where \(K[V]_d\) is the space of homogeneous degree-\(d\) polynomials. \(G\) acts on functions by \((g\cdot f)(v)=f(g^{-1}v)\); this preserves the grading. The invariant ring is \(K[V]^G=\{f: g\cdot f=f\ \forall g\}\), itself graded: \(K[V]^G=\bigoplus_d K[V]^G_d\).

For a graded vector space \(M=\bigoplus_d M_d\) with finite-dimensional pieces, its Hilbert series is \(H(M,t)=\sum_{d\ge0}\dim(M_d)\,t^d\). The goal is \(H(K[V]^G,t)\).

2. The graded trace of a single \(g\)

This computation feeds both Molien's formula and (later) the Koszul proof.

Lemma 1 — graded trace For \(g\in G\) with action matrix \(\rho(g)\) on \(V\), \[ \sum_{d\ge0}\tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big)\,t^d\ =\ \frac{1}{\det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)^{-1}\big)} . \]
Proof. Since \(G\) is finite and \(\operatorname{char}K=0\), \(g\) is diagonalizable on \(V\) (its minimal polynomial divides \(x^{|g|}-1\), which is separable). Extend scalars to \(\C\); let \(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n\) be the eigenvalues of \(\rho(g)\) on \(V\), so the dual eigenvalues on \(V^*\) are \(\mu_i=\lambda_i^{-1}\). Pick an eigenbasis \(y_1,\dots,y_n\) of \(V^*\) for \(g\), so \(g\cdot y_i=\mu_i y_i\); the monomials \(y_1^{a_1}\cdots y_n^{a_n}\) with \(\sum_i a_i=d\) form a basis of \(\Sym^d(V^*)=K[V]_d\). Since \(g\) acts as an algebra automorphism, \[ g\cdot\big(y_1^{a_1}\cdots y_n^{a_n}\big)=(g\cdot y_1)^{a_1}\cdots(g\cdot y_n)^{a_n} =\big(\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}\big)\,y_1^{a_1}\cdots y_n^{a_n}, \] each monomial is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}\); as they form a basis, these products are exactly the eigenvalues of \(g\) on \(K[V]_d\), so \[ \tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big)=\!\!\sum_{a_1+\cdots+a_n=d}\!\!\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}=h_d(\mu), \] the complete homogeneous symmetric polynomial \(h_d(\mu)\) — the sum of all degree-\(d\) monomials in \(\mu_1,\dots,\mu_n\).

Its generating function. Weight each monomial by \(t^{\deg}\): a degree-\(d\) monomial \(\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}\) (with \(\sum_i a_i=d\)) picks up \(t^{a_1+\cdots+a_n}=\prod_i(\mu_i t)^{a_i}\). So summing \(h_d\,t^d\) over all \(d\) amounts to ranging over every exponent tuple \((a_1,\dots,a_n)\in\N^n\); because the entries \(a_1,\dots,a_n\) are chosen independently, the sum factors into \(n\) geometric series, one per variable: \[ \sum_{d\ge0} h_d(\mu)\,t^d \;=\!\!\sum_{a_1,\dots,a_n\ge0}\ \prod_{i=1}^{n}(\mu_i t)^{a_i} \;=\;\prod_{i=1}^{n}\Big(\sum_{a\ge0}(\mu_i t)^{a}\Big) \;=\;\prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{1-\mu_i t} \;=\;\frac{1}{\det\!\big(I-t\,g|_{V^*}\big)} . \] The middle equality is the distributive law \(\prod_i\sum_{a}=\sum_{(a_i)}\prod_i\) (a Cauchy product of the \(n\) series); each factor sums the geometric series \(\sum_{a\ge0}(\mu_i t)^a=\tfrac1{1-\mu_i t}\) (as a formal power series, equivalently for small \(|t|\)). The final equality holds because the \(\mu_i\) are precisely the eigenvalues of \(g\) on \(V^*\), so \(\det(I-t\,g|_{V^*})=\prod_i(1-\mu_i t)\). Finally, since \(\mu_i=\lambda_i^{-1}\), \(\ \det(I-t\,g|_{V^*})=\prod_i(1-\lambda_i^{-1}t)=\det(I-t\,\rho(g)^{-1})\).

3. Molien's formula

Theorem (Molien) \[ H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}\frac{1}{\det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)\big)} . \]
Proof. The Reynolds operator \(\Rey=\frac1{|G|}\sum_{g}g\) acts on each \(K[V]_d\) and is a projection onto invariants: \(\Rey f=f\) for invariant \(f\), and \(\Rey f\) is always invariant since left-multiplying the sum by \(h\in G\) merely permutes it. A projection's trace equals the dimension of its image, so \[ \dim K[V]^G_d=\tr\!\big(\Rey\mid K[V]_d\big)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_{g}\tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big). \] Multiply by \(t^d\), sum, and use Lemma 1: \(H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_g\frac1{\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})}\). Reindexing \(g\mapsto g^{-1}\) (a bijection of \(G\)) replaces \(\rho(g)^{-1}\) by \(\rho(g)\), giving the stated form.

Each \(\det(I-t\rho(g))\) is a degree-\(n\) polynomial with value \(1\) at \(t=0\); Molien thus writes \(H\) as a rational function whose denominators are dictated by the group elements. That is the lever for the degree bound.

4. Separating invariants and the zero set

Definition A set \(S\subseteq K[V]^G\) is separating if it distinguishes every pair the whole invariant ring distinguishes: for \(u,v\in V\), if \(f(u)=f(v)\) for all \(f\in S\) then \(f(u)=f(v)\) for all \(f\in K[V]^G\). Equivalently, \(S\) separates the \(G\)-orbits. \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) is the least \(d\) admitting a separating set of invariants of degree \(\le d\).
Lemma 2 — separating \(\Rightarrow\) trivial zero set If \(S=\{f_1,\dots,f_r\}\) are homogeneous separating invariants, then \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\).
Proof. Each \(f_i\) is homogeneous of positive degree, so \(f_i(0)=0\); thus \(0\) is a common zero. For \(v\neq0\), the orbit \(G\!\cdot\!v\) is finite, hence closed, and disjoint from \(\{0\}\); for a finite group \(K[V]^G\) separates disjoint orbits, so some invariant \(h\) has \(h(v)\neq h(0)=0\). As \(S\) is separating and \(h(v)\neq h(0)\), some \(f_i\) has \(f_i(v)\neq f_i(0)=0\). Hence \(v\notin V(f_1,\dots,f_r)\).

5. Hilbert's finiteness lemma

This upgrades "zero set \(\{0\}\)" to module-finiteness — the hypothesis Harm's divisibility theorem is phrased with (though the Koszul proof will only need the zero-set condition itself).

Definition — finite module A module \(M\) over a ring \(A\) is finite (= finitely generated) if there are \(b_1,\dots,b_s\in M\) with \(M=A\,b_1+\cdots+A\,b_s\): every element is \(a_1b_1+\cdots+a_sb_s\) with coefficients \(a_i\in A\). It is the ring analogue of a finite-dimensional vector space — a finite spanning set, but over a ring, so in general there are generators without a basis.
Module-finite vs algebra-finite. Being finitely generated as an algebra is much weaker, because algebras also allow products of generators. \(K[x]\) is algebra-finite over \(K\) (one generator \(x\)) but not module-finite (as a \(K\)-module it is the infinite-dimensional span of \(1,x,x^2,\dots\)); it is module-finite over \(K[x^2]\), with generators \(\{1,x\}\): every polynomial is \(p(x^2)\cdot1+q(x^2)\cdot x\). Closer to our setting, \(K[x_1,x_2]\) is module-finite over \(K[e_1,e_2]\) with generators \(\{1,x_1\}\).
Why it matters here. If \(K[V]^G=\sum_j A\,b_j\) with \(A=K[f_1,\dots,f_r]\) and homogeneous \(b_j\), each generator contributes \(t^{\deg b_j}\) times the series of \(A\), so \[ H(K[V]^G,t)\ =\ \frac{q(t)}{\prod_{i=1}^r(1-t^{d_i})},\qquad q\in\Z[t] \] — comparing this rigid shape with Molien's universal denominator \(p(t)\) is what forces \(p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\) on the Machinery page.
Lemma 3 — Hilbert Let \(f_1,\dots,f_r\in K[V]\) be homogeneous of positive degree with \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\) over \(\overline K\). Then \(K[V]\) is a finite module over \(A:=K[f_1,\dots,f_r]\); if the \(f_i\) are invariant, so is \(K[V]^G\).
Proof. Let \(\mm=K[V]_+\) and \(I=(f_1,\dots,f_r)\). Since \(V(I)=\{0\}=V(\mm)\), the Nullstellensatz gives \(\sqrt I=\mm\). Hence \(K[V]/I\) is graded Artinian with a single homogeneous prime, so \(\dim_K K[V]/I<\infty\); choose homogeneous \(b_1,\dots,b_s\) whose classes are a \(K\)-basis. By graded Nakayama, \(K[V]=\sum_j A\,b_j\): were \(N=K[V]/\sum_j Ab_j\neq0\), take its lowest nonzero degree; every element is an \(A\)-combination of the \(b_j\) modulo \(I\subseteq A_+K[V]\), pushing that component to strictly lower degree — contradiction. So \(K[V]\) is finite over \(A\); as \(A\subseteq K[V]^G\subseteq K[V]\) with \(A\) Noetherian, the submodule \(K[V]^G\) is finite over \(A\) too.

6. Generation vs separation: \(\beta\) and \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\)

Definition \(\beta(K[V]^G)\) is the least \(D\) such that the invariants of degree \(\le D\) generate \(K[V]^G\) as a \(K\)-algebra.
Lemma 4 — separation is at most generation \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V]^G)\ \le\ \beta(K[V]^G)\).
Proof. Let \(g_1,\dots,g_m\) be homogeneous generators of degree \(\le\beta\). Every invariant \(f\) is a polynomial \(F(g_1,\dots,g_m)\); if all \(g_i\) agree at \(u,v\), then so does \(f\). Hence the generators are a separating set.

So any upper bound on the generation degree \(\beta\) bounds \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) — this is how the note's upper bounds are produced. The converse can fail badly: separation may be much cheaper than generation.

Example — separation \(\ll\) generation Separation is always bounded by \(|G|\), in every characteristic [DK15]: for a linear form \(\ell\), the coefficients of \(t\mapsto\prod_{g\in G}\big(t+\ell(g\cdot v)\big)\) are invariants of degree \(\le|G|\), and letting \(\ell\) vary they detect the multiset \(\{\ell(g\cdot v)\}_g\), hence the orbit — so \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le|G|\) always. Generation is not: in characteristic \(p\), for \(G=\Z/p\) acting on \(m\) copies of its 2-dimensional indecomposable representation over \(\mathbb{F}_p\), the invariant ring needs a generator of degree \(\ge m(p-1)\) (Richman 1990) — unbounded as \(m\) grows, while \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le|G|=p\) stays constant. The generation/separation gap can be arbitrarily large.
In characteristic \(0\) Noether's bound \(\beta\le|G|\) caps the gap, but generation bounds still overshoot: on \(\R^n\), Göbel gives \(\binom n2\) while \(\beta=\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}=n\) (§7). Whether the same overshoot happens for \(V_n=S^2(\R^n)\) — i.e. whether \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) is really \(\Theta(n^2)\) rather than the Göbel-type \(\Theta(n^4)\) — is exactly Audit direction D1.

7. Göbel's bound (the one imported proof)

Theorem — Göbel 1995 If \(G\le S_m\) acts on \(K[x_1,\dots,x_m]\) by permuting the variables (any field \(K\)), then \[ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\ \le\ \beta\big(K[x_1,\dots,x_m]^G\big)\ \le\ \max\Big\{m,\ \binom{m}{2}\Big\}. \]
Refined version — [DK15, Cor. 3.10.9] If the variables split into \(G\)-orbits of lengths \(m_1,\dots,m_s\), then \[ \beta\big(K[x_1,\dots,x_m]^G\big)\ \le\ \max\Big\{m_1,\dots,m_s,\ \binom{m_1}{2}+\cdots+\binom{m_s}{2}\Big\}. \]
Proof idea (sketch — the only result this site imports without full proof). Invariants are spanned by orbit sums of monomials \(x^e\). Call \(e\) special if, within each variable-orbit, its sorted exponents form a gap-free staircase ending at \(0\) (steps \(\le 1\)). Göbel's induction rewrites any non-special orbit sum in terms of lower ones and the per-orbit products \(\prod_{x\in\text{orbit}}x\) (degree \(m_i\)); a staircase on an orbit of length \(m_i\) has degree at most \(0+1+\cdots+(m_i-1)=\binom{m_i}{2}\). Note Göbel can overshoot: for \(S_n\) on \(\R^n\) it gives \(\binom{n}{2}\), while in fact \(\beta=n\) (elementary symmetric polynomials).
Carry forward. Molien: \(H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_g\frac1{\det(I-t\rho(g))}\). Separating invariants have zero set \(\{0\}\) (Lemma 2), hence make \(K[V]^G\) module-finite over the subring they generate (Lemma 3) — this feeds the lower-bound machinery. In the other direction, \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le\beta\) (Lemma 4) and Göbel's bound feed the Upper Bound page.
