Background

Everything needed to state Harm's theorems, assuming only representation theory, group theory, and (multi)linear algebra. Two results are load-bearing: Molien's formula and Hilbert's finiteness lemma. Both are proved.

1. The invariant ring and its Hilbert series

Let \(G\) be a finite group and \(V\cong K^n\) a representation over a field \(K\) of characteristic \(0\), with \(\rho(g)\in GL(V)\) the action matrices. Polynomial functions on \(V\) form the graded ring \[ K[V]=\Sym(V^*)=\bigoplus_{d\ge 0}K[V]_d,\qquad K[V]_d=\Sym^d(V^*), \] where \(K[V]_d\) is the space of homogeneous degree-\(d\) polynomials. \(G\) acts on functions by \((g\cdot f)(v)=f(g^{-1}v)\); this preserves the grading. The invariant ring is \(K[V]^G=\{f: g\cdot f=f\ \forall g\}\), itself graded: \(K[V]^G=\bigoplus_d K[V]^G_d\).

For a graded vector space \(M=\bigoplus_d M_d\) with finite-dimensional pieces, its Hilbert series is \(H(M,t)=\sum_{d\ge0}\dim(M_d)\,t^d\). The goal is \(H(K[V]^G,t)\).

2. The graded trace of a single \(g\)

This computation feeds both Molien's formula and (later) the Koszul proof.

Lemma 1 — graded trace For \(g\in G\) with action matrix \(\rho(g)\) on \(V\), \[ \sum_{d\ge0}\tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big)\,t^d\ =\ \frac{1}{\det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)^{-1}\big)} . \]
Proof. Since \(G\) is finite and \(\operatorname{char}K=0\), \(g\) is diagonalizable on \(V\) (its minimal polynomial divides \(x^{|g|}-1\), which is separable). Extend scalars to \(\C\); let \(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n\) be the eigenvalues of \(\rho(g)\) on \(V\), so the dual eigenvalues on \(V^*\) are \(\mu_i=\lambda_i^{-1}\). Then \(g\) acts on \(K[V]_d=\Sym^d(V^*)\) with eigenvalues the degree-\(d\) monomials \(\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}\), \(\sum a_i=d\), so \[ \tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big)=\!\!\sum_{a_1+\cdots+a_n=d}\!\!\mu_1^{a_1}\cdots\mu_n^{a_n}=h_d(\mu), \] the complete homogeneous symmetric polynomial. Its generating function is \(\sum_d h_d(\mu)t^d=\prod_{i}\frac1{1-\mu_i t}=\frac1{\det(I-t\,g|_{V^*})}\), and \(\det(I-t\,g|_{V^*})=\prod_i(1-\lambda_i^{-1}t)=\det(I-t\,\rho(g)^{-1})\).

3. Molien's formula

Theorem (Molien) \[ H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}\frac{1}{\det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)\big)} . \]
Proof. The Reynolds operator \(\Rey=\frac1{|G|}\sum_{g}g\) acts on each \(K[V]_d\) and is a projection onto invariants: \(\Rey f=f\) for invariant \(f\), and \(\Rey f\) is always invariant since left-multiplying the sum by \(h\in G\) merely permutes it. A projection's trace equals the dimension of its image, so \[ \dim K[V]^G_d=\tr\!\big(\Rey\mid K[V]_d\big)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_{g}\tr\!\big(g\mid K[V]_d\big). \] Multiply by \(t^d\), sum, and use Lemma 1: \(H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_g\frac1{\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})}\). Reindexing \(g\mapsto g^{-1}\) (a bijection of \(G\)) replaces \(\rho(g)^{-1}\) by \(\rho(g)\), giving the stated form.

Each \(\det(I-t\rho(g))\) is a degree-\(n\) polynomial with value \(1\) at \(t=0\); Molien thus writes \(H\) as a rational function whose denominators are dictated by the group elements. That is the lever for the degree bound.

4. Separating invariants and the zero set

Definition A set \(S\subseteq K[V]^G\) is separating if it distinguishes every pair the whole invariant ring distinguishes: for \(u,v\in V\), if \(f(u)=f(v)\) for all \(f\in S\) then \(f(u)=f(v)\) for all \(f\in K[V]^G\). Equivalently, \(S\) separates the \(G\)-orbits. \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) is the least \(d\) admitting a separating set of invariants of degree \(\le d\).
Lemma 2 — separating \(\Rightarrow\) trivial zero set If \(S=\{f_1,\dots,f_r\}\) are homogeneous separating invariants, then \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\).
Proof. Each \(f_i\) is homogeneous of positive degree, so \(f_i(0)=0\); thus \(0\) is a common zero. For \(v\neq0\), the orbit \(G\!\cdot\!v\) is finite, hence closed, and disjoint from \(\{0\}\); for a finite group \(K[V]^G\) separates disjoint orbits, so some invariant \(h\) has \(h(v)\neq h(0)=0\). As \(S\) is separating and \(h(v)\neq h(0)\), some \(f_i\) has \(f_i(v)\neq f_i(0)=0\). Hence \(v\notin V(f_1,\dots,f_r)\).

5. Hilbert's finiteness lemma

This upgrades "zero set \(\{0\}\)" to module-finiteness — the hypothesis Harm's divisibility theorem is phrased with (though the Koszul proof will only need the zero-set condition itself).

Lemma 3 — Hilbert Let \(f_1,\dots,f_r\in K[V]\) be homogeneous of positive degree with \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\) over \(\overline K\). Then \(K[V]\) is a finite module over \(A:=K[f_1,\dots,f_r]\); if the \(f_i\) are invariant, so is \(K[V]^G\).
Proof. Let \(\mm=K[V]_+\) and \(I=(f_1,\dots,f_r)\). Since \(V(I)=\{0\}=V(\mm)\), the Nullstellensatz gives \(\sqrt I=\mm\). Hence \(K[V]/I\) is graded Artinian with a single homogeneous prime, so \(\dim_K K[V]/I<\infty\); choose homogeneous \(b_1,\dots,b_s\) whose classes are a \(K\)-basis. By graded Nakayama, \(K[V]=\sum_j A\,b_j\): were \(N=K[V]/\sum_j Ab_j\neq0\), take its lowest nonzero degree; every element is an \(A\)-combination of the \(b_j\) modulo \(I\subseteq A_+K[V]\), pushing that component to strictly lower degree — contradiction. So \(K[V]\) is finite over \(A\); as \(A\subseteq K[V]^G\subseteq K[V]\) with \(A\) Noetherian, the submodule \(K[V]^G\) is finite over \(A\) too.
Carry forward. Molien: \(H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_g\frac1{\det(I-t\rho(g))}\). Separating invariants have zero set \(\{0\}\) (Lemma 2), hence make \(K[V]^G\) module-finite over the subring they generate (Lemma 3). Next: turn this into a divisibility constraint on their degrees.
