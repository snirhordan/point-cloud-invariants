Upper bounds: separation in degree \(\sim n^4/8\)

The note's v2 adds the other side of the story: separating invariants for \(S_n\) on \(V_n=S^2(\R^n)\) exist in degree \(O(n^4)\) — first by an explicit Lagrange-interpolation construction (\(\sim n^4/2\)), then improved to \(\sim n^4/8\) via Göbel's bound. Together with the lower bound: \((\tfrac14-o(1))\,n^2\ \le\ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})\ \le\ (\tfrac18+o(1))\,n^4\).

1. The Lagrange construction (\(\sim n^4/2\))

Theorem — note §2 Let \(N=\binom{n+1}{2}=\frac{n^2+n}{2}=\dim V_n\). The invariant polynomials of degree \(\le N(2N-1)=\frac{(n^2+n-1)(n^2+n)}{2}\) separate the \(S_n\)-orbits on \(V_n\).
Proof. Let \(A,B\in V_n\) lie in different orbits; we build an invariant \(P\) with \(P(A)\neq P(B)\). Let \(\Lambda=\{A_{kl}:k\le l\}\cup\{B_{kl}:k\le l\}\) be the set of values of the entries of \(A\) and \(B\); \(|\Lambda|\le 2N\). For each \(i\le j\) define the Lagrange polynomial \[ \ell_{ij}(t)\ =\ \prod_{\lambda\in\Lambda\setminus\{A_{ij}\}}\frac{t-\lambda}{A_{ij}-\lambda}, \qquad \deg\ell_{ij}=|\Lambda|-1\le 2N-1, \] so \(\ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1\) and \(\ell_{ij}\) vanishes at every other value in \(\Lambda\). Set \[ P(X)\ =\ \sum_{\tau\in S_n}\ \prod_{i\le j}\ \ell_{ij}\big(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\big). \] Invariance: replacing \(X\) by \(\sigma\cdot X\) replaces \(\tau\) by \(\sigma^{-1}\tau\) inside the sum — a reindexing of \(S_n\). \(P(A)\ge1\): every argument \(A_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\) is an entry-value of \(A\), hence a node, so each factor is \(0\) or \(1\) and each \(\tau\)-term lies in \(\{0,1\}\); the identity term is \(\prod_{i\le j}\ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1\). \(P(B)=0\): since \(B\notin S_n\!\cdot\!A\), for every \(\tau\) there is a pair \(i\le j\) with \(B_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\neq A_{ij}\); that argument is a node different from \(A_{ij}\), so \(\ell_{ij}\) vanishes and the whole \(\tau\)-term is \(0\). Finally \(\deg P\le N(2N-1)\).
Step-by-step version — every move justified

Step 0 — what \(\ell_{ij}\) does on nodes. Each denominator \(A_{ij}-\lambda\neq0\) since the product skips \(\lambda=A_{ij}\). At \(t=A_{ij}\) every factor is \(1\), so \(\ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1\); at any other node \(\mu\in\Lambda\) the factor with \(\lambda=\mu\) is \(0\). So restricted to \(\Lambda\), \(\ell_{ij}\) is the indicator of the value \(A_{ij}\). Two cautions the whole proof leans on: (i) this is about values, not positions — if another entry happens to equal the number \(A_{ij}\), the indicator correctly returns \(1\) there; (ii) off \(\Lambda\) the polynomial is arbitrary, so the proof must only ever evaluate \(\ell_{ij}\) at nodes.

Step 1 — \(X_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\) makes sense. \(\tau(i)>\tau(j)\) can happen, but \(X\) is symmetric, so this is the entry at the unordered pair \(\{\tau(i),\tau(j)\}\). Each \(\tau\)-term asks: does \(X\), relabeled by \(\tau\), look like \(A\) at every position?

Step 2 — invariance. With \((\sigma\cdot X)_{ab}=X_{\sigma^{-1}(a),\sigma^{-1}(b)}\), substituting gives arguments \(X_{\sigma^{-1}\tau(i),\sigma^{-1}\tau(j)}\); the change of variable \(\tau'=\sigma^{-1}\tau\) — a bijection of \(S_n\) — restores \(P(X)\). Invariance is pure reindexing.

Step 3 — \(P(A)\ge1\), four moves. (1) Every factor is a node evaluation: \(A_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\) is an entry-value of \(A\), hence in \(\Lambda\) — this is why \(\Lambda\) was built from the entries of both matrices. (2) Each factor is \(0\) or \(1\) by Step 0. (3) Each \(\tau\)-term is \(0\) or \(1\): finite product of factors in \(\{0,1\}\). (4) Identity term \(=1\), nothing cancels: for \(\tau=\mathrm{id}\) every factor is \(\ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1\); all other terms are \(\ge0\), so \(P(A)=1+\sum(\ge0)\ge1\). Nonnegativity is what rules out cancellation. (Terms for symmetries of \(A\) may also equal \(1\) — harmless.)

Step 4 — \(P(B)=0\), four moves. Fix \(\tau\). (1) Witness position exists: if \(B_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}=A_{ij}\) for all \(i\le j\), then with \(\sigma=\tau^{-1}\) in Step 2's action, \((\tau^{-1}\cdot B)_{ij}=B_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}=A_{ij}\), i.e. \(A=\tau^{-1}\cdot B\) — same orbit, contradiction. So some \(i\le j\) has \(B_{\tau(i),\tau(j)}\neq A_{ij}\). (2) The witness argument is a node: it is an entry-value of \(B\), hence in \(\Lambda\). (3) The witness factor vanishes: a node whose value differs from \(A_{ij}\) lies in \(\Lambda\setminus\{A_{ij}\}\) — exactly the root set of \(\ell_{ij}\). (4) One zero factor kills the \(\tau\)-term; \(\tau\) was arbitrary, so \(P(B)=0\).

Step 5 — degree and conclusion. Each \(\tau\)-term is a product of \(N\) factors of degree \(\le|\Lambda|-1\le2N-1\), so \(\deg P\le N(2N-1)\), and \(P(A)\ge1>0=P(B)\). \(P\) depends on the pair \((A,B)\) — allowed: \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) only requires that for every pair of distinct orbits some invariant of that degree separates.

The two design decisions carrying everything: evaluate only at nodes (Steps 3.1, 4.2 — guaranteed by putting all entries of both \(A\) and \(B\) into \(\Lambda\)), and nonnegativity on nodes (Step 3.4). Drop either and the argument collapses — which is exactly how the point-cloud lemma's proof broke (finding #4).

Two reading notes. (i) \(P\) depends on the pair \((A,B)\) — that is all \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) requires: it makes the set of all invariants of degree \(\le N(2N-1)\) a separating set. (ii) The proof is correct as written in the note; it is also the template whose miscopy breaks the point-cloud lemma — see Point Clouds §3.

2. Göbel refinement (\(\sim n^4/8\))

View \(S_n\) as a subgroup of the full permutation group of the \(\binom{n+1}{2}\) basis vectors \(\{f_{ij}\}\) of \(V_n\) (see Lower Bound §1). The variables split into exactly two \(S_n\)-orbits: the loops \(\{f_{ii}\}\) (length \(n\)) and the edges \(\{f_{ij}: i<j\}\) (length \(\binom{n}{2}\)). The refined Göbel bound (Background §7) applies verbatim:

Corollary — corrected For \(n\ge3\), \[ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\big(K[V_n]^{S_n}\big)\ \le\ \max\left\{n,\ \binom{n}{2},\ \binom{n}{2}+\binom{\binom{n}{2}}{2}\right\} \ =\ \binom{\binom{n}{2}+1}{2}\ =\ \frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}. \] (For \(n=2\) the maximum is \(n=2\).)
Proof. The refined Göbel bound with orbit lengths \(m_1=n\), \(m_2=\binom n2\) gives \(\beta\le\max\{n,\binom n2,\binom n2+\binom{\binom n2}{2}\}\), and \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\le\beta\) (Background, Lemma 4). With \(m=\binom n2=\frac{n^2-n}2\): \(m+\binom m2=\frac{m(m+1)}2=\binom{m+1}2 =\frac12\cdot\frac{n^2-n}{2}\cdot\frac{n^2-n+2}{2}=\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\), which dominates the other two entries for \(n\ge3\).
Error in the note (finding #3 — arithmetic slip). The note states the bound as \(\frac18(n^2-n)(n^2-n-1)+\frac12(n^2-n)=\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+3)}{8}\). But \(\binom m2=\frac12\cdot\frac{n^2-n}{2}\big(\frac{n^2-n}{2}-1\big)=\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n-2)}{8}\) — the factor should be \(n^2-n-2\), not \(n^2-n-1\). Correct total: \(\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\). Sanity check \(n=3\): true value \(\binom32+\binom32=6=\binom42\); the note's formula gives \(\frac{6\cdot9}{8}=\frac{27}{4}\) — not even an integer. (Same at \(n=4\): \(21\) vs \(22.5\).) Harmless slip: the corrected bound is slightly stronger; both are \(\sim n^4/8\).
The sandwich. \[ \Big(\tfrac14-o(1)\Big)n^2\ \le\ \beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\big(K[V_n]^{S_n}\big)\ \le\ \frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\ \sim\ \frac{n^4}{8}. \] Quadratic below, quartic above — a gap of order \(n^2\). Since Göbel bounds generation (which can overshoot separation, as on \(\R^n\)), the truth may well be closer to \(n^2\); see Audit, direction D1.
