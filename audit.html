B. Improvement directions

D1. Close the graph gap: \(n^2/4\) vs \(n^4/8\)

The lower-bound technique is exhausted at \(n^2/4\) on \(V_n\). Every factor of \(\det(I-t\rho(\sigma))\) is \(1-t^{|O|}\) for an orbit \(O\) of \(\langle\sigma\rangle\) on unordered pairs, and orbit sizes are \(\lcm(a,b)\le ab\le\lfloor n/2\rfloor\lceil n/2\rceil\approx n^2/4\). So no choice of \(\sigma\) forces a single degree beyond \(\sim n^2/4\) by the cyclotomic route: pushing the lower bound past \(n^2/4\) needs genuinely new obstructions (e.g. analyzing the numerator \(q(t)\) of the Hilbert series, not just the denominator).
Multiplicities bound counts, not just the max. Since cross-cycle orbits give \((1-t^{\lcm(a,b)})^{\gcd(a,b)}\), the same divisibility forces \(\Phi_{\lcm(a,b)}\) to divide at least \(\gcd(a,b)\) of the factors \(1-t^{d_i}\): at least \(\gcd(a,b)\) separating invariants must have degree divisible by \(\lcm(a,b)\). A quantitative refinement worth stating.
The upper bound measures the wrong thing. Göbel bounds generation; separation can be cheaper (on \(\R^n\): Göbel gives \(\binom n2\), truth is \(n\)). The natural question: is \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})=\Theta(n^2)\), i.e. is the lower bound tight?

D2. Repair the \(O(d^2n^2)\) point-cloud upper bound

The Cholesky idea fails because the parametrization is neither polynomial nor \(S_n\)-compatible (finding #5). Candidate repairs: (i) use \(X\mapsto X^TX\) itself as the polynomial parametrization of \(\mathcal G_d\) — it has the right dimension count and \(S_n\) acts on the \(n\) columns of \(X\) by permutation; the difficulty then moves to handling the residual \(O(d)\)-redundancy inside the interpolation; (ii) interpolate directly on \(\mathcal G_d\) with \(S_n\)-invariant polynomial families adapted to low rank; (iii) separate generic configurations cheaply (rank-\(d\), distinct entries) and handle degenerate strata by induction on rank. Target: \(D(n,d)=\mathrm{poly}(d)\cdot n^2\).

D3. Lower bounds in the point-cloud setting

Nothing is currently known for \(D(n,d)\), \(d<n\). By the FFT reduction (Point Clouds §2), \(D(n,d)=2\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[\mathcal G_d]^{S_n})\) — so the precise open problem is a degree lower bound for \(S_n\)-separation on the rank-\(\le d\) PSD cone, a subvariety, where the universal-denominator machinery (built for linear actions on all of \(V\)) does not apply as-is. Two routes: extend the denominator theory to Hilbert series of \(K[\mathcal G_d]^{S_n}\), or work upstairs on \(\R^{d\times n}\) with the reductive group \(O(d)\times S_n\) via the Molien–Weyl integral.

D4. Consequences for MACE / invariant architectures

(i) The proven \(D(n,3)\le\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{4}\sim n^4/4\) gives a concrete, finite target degree for complete invariant feature maps of \(n\)-point clouds — e.g. an expansion-order target for ACE-type constructions; a repaired D2 would lower it to \(O(n^2)\). (ii) The graph lower bound \(\Omega(n^2)\) warns that bounded-degree invariant readouts cannot be complete on all configurations. (iii) The \(q\)-separated escape hatch: the lower-bound machinery requires separation on all of \(V\) (it uses the orbit \(\{0\}\) and arbitrary matrices). Universality programs that assume compact, collision-free, \(q\)-separated configurations only need separation on a restricted subset — the \(\Omega(n^2)\) barrier is not established there, and cheaper invariants may suffice. Whether a degree bound independent of \(q\) exists is exactly the program's open question. (iv) Via Gram = distances (§6), completeness is relabeling-invariant completeness of the distance matrix; known counterexamples where low-order distance summaries fail to distinguish clouds are the qualitative shadow of these quantitative degree costs.

Status summary. Proved: \((\tfrac14-o(1))n^2\le\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}(K[V_n]^{S_n})\le\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{8}\) and \(D(n,d)\le\frac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{4}\). Open: the \(O(d^2n^2)\) point-cloud upper bound (claimed, proof gapped), any point-cloud lower bound, and the true order of \(\beta_{\mathrm{sep}}\) between \(n^2\) and \(n^4\).
