Geometric graphs: the first-\(d\)-rows theorem

A repaired upper bound for point-cloud separation, replacing the broken Cholesky argument (finding #5): the first \(d\) rows of the Gram matrix are exactly \(N_d=dn-\binom d2\) honest entries — polynomial, and permuted by \(S_n\) as entries — and they determine a rank-\(\le d\) PSD matrix via the Schur-complement completion identity. Result: \(D(n,d)\le 2N_d(n^2+n-1)\sim 2dn^3\), fully proved below and validated in exact arithmetic. Bound chain: \(n^4/4\) (proved earlier) \(\to\) \(\mathbf{2dn^3}\) (this page) \(\to\) \(4d^2n^2\) (still open).

1. Statement

Theorem — first-\(d\)-rows upper bound For all \(n\ge2\), \(1\le d\le n\), the \(O(d)\times S_n\)-separation degree on \(\R^{d\times n}\) satisfies \[ D(n,d)\ \le\ 2\,N_d\,(n^2+n-1),\qquad N_d=dn-\binom d2 . \] Asymptotically \(D(n,d)=O(dn^3)\); at \(d=3\): \(D(n,3)\le 2(3n-3)(n^2+n-1)\sim 6n^3\).

2. Step 1 — Gram reduction

As on Point Clouds §2 (proved there): \(X,Y\in\R^{d\times n}\) lie in different \(O(d)\times S_n\)-orbits iff \(A=X^TX\), \(B=Y^TY\) lie in different \(S_n\)-orbits in the cone \(\mathcal G_d\) of PSD rank-\(\le d\) matrices; and by Weyl's FFT ([[weyl-fft-orthogonal]]) any \(S_n\)-invariant polynomial \(Q\) in the Gram entries pulls back to the \(O(d)\times S_n\)-invariant \(p(X)=Q(X^TX)\) with \(\deg p=2\deg Q\). It therefore suffices to separate \(A\) from \(B\) by an \(S_n\)-invariant polynomial on \(V_n=S^2(\R^n)\) of degree \(\le N_d(n^2+n-1)\).

3. Step 2 — rank case-split

Lemma R — unequal ranks are cheap If \(\rank A\neq\rank B\), some \(\tr(G^k)\), \(k\le n\), separates them; its pullback \(\tr((X^TX)^k)\) has degree \(2k\le2n\).
Proof. The power sums \(\tr(G^k)=\sum_i\lambda_i(G)^k\), \(k=1,\dots,n\), determine the elementary symmetric polynomials of the eigenvalues (Newton's identities, [[newton-identities-power-sums]]), hence the spectrum as a multiset, hence \(\rank=\#\{\lambda_i\neq0\}\). If all \(\tr(A^k)=\tr(B^k)\) then \(\rank A=\rank B\); contrapositive gives the separator. Each \(\tr(G^k)\) is \(S_n\)-invariant (conjugation by a permutation matrix is a similarity).

Since \(2n\le 2N_d(n^2+n-1)\), we may from now on assume \(\rank A=\rank B=r\le d\).

4. Step 3 — choice of representative

Lemma P — invertible principal block A PSD matrix \(G\) of rank \(r\) has an invertible \(r\times r\) principal submatrix. Consequently some permutation \(\tau_0\) makes the leading \(r\times r\) block of \(\tau_0\cdot G\) invertible.
Proof. Write \(G=Z^TZ\) with \(Z\in\R^{r\times n}\) (possible: PSD of rank \(r\)). Some \(r\) columns \(z_{i_1},\dots,z_{i_r}\) of \(Z\) are linearly independent; the principal submatrix of \(G\) on \(\{i_1,\dots,i_r\}\) is their Gram matrix, which is invertible. Let \(\tau_0\) move \(i_1,\dots,i_r\) to positions \(1,\dots,r\).

Separating \(A\) from \(B\) is equivalent to separating \(\tau_0\cdot A\) from \(B\) (orbits are unchanged, and the separating polynomial is allowed to depend on the pair). WLOG \(A\)'s leading \(r\times r\) block \(A_{11}\) is invertible. No assumption on \(B\) is needed.

5. Step 4 — the completion identity

Lemma C — first \(r\) rows determine a rank-\(\le r\) PSD matrix Let \(G\in V_n\) be PSD with \(\rank G\le r\) and invertible leading block \(G_{11}\in\R^{r\times r}\). Writing \(G=\begin{pmatrix}G_{11}&G_{12}\\G_{12}^T&G_{22}\end{pmatrix}\), \[ G_{22}=G_{12}^T\,G_{11}^{-1}\,G_{12}. \]
Proof. The congruence (Aitken block factorization) \[ G=\begin{pmatrix}I&0\\G_{12}^TG_{11}^{-1}&I\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}G_{11}&0\\0&S\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}I&G_{11}^{-1}G_{12}\\0&I\end{pmatrix},\qquad S:=G_{22}-G_{12}^TG_{11}^{-1}G_{12}, \] is verified by block multiplication. Congruence preserves rank and positive semidefiniteness, so \(\rank G=\rank G_{11}+\rank S=r+\rank S\) (Guttman) and \(S\succeq0\) (Albert/Haynsworth; [[schur-complement-psd-rank]]). From \(\rank G\le r\): \(\rank S=0\), and the only PSD matrix of rank \(0\) is \(S=0\).

6. Step 5 — the witness lemma

Let \(\mathcal P_r=\{(i,j):1\le i\le r,\ i\le j\le n\}\) be the positions of the first \(r\) rows (upper part); \(|\mathcal P_r|=\sum_{i=1}^r(n-i+1)=rn-\binom r2=N_r\le N_d\).

Lemma W — the witness sits in the first \(r\) rows Let \(A,B\in\mathcal G_d\) with \(\rank A=\rank B=r\), \(A_{11}\) invertible, and \(A,B\) in different \(S_n\)-orbits. Then for every \(\sigma\in S_n\) there is a position \((i,j)\in\mathcal P_r\) with \((\sigma\cdot B)_{ij}\neq A_{ij}\).
Proof. Suppose \(\sigma\cdot B\) agrees with \(A\) at every position of \(\mathcal P_r\); we derive a contradiction. Agreement on \(\mathcal P_r\) means the two matrices have the same first \(r\) rows (by symmetry, also the same first \(r\) columns); in particular \((\sigma B)_{11}=A_{11}\) is invertible, so \(\rank(\sigma B)\ge r\). But \(\rank(\sigma B)=\rank B=r\) (similarity). Thus \(\sigma\cdot B\) is PSD of rank exactly \(r\) with invertible leading \(r\times r\) block, and so is \(A\); Lemma C applies to both: \[ (\sigma B)_{22}=(\sigma B)_{12}^T(\sigma B)_{11}^{-1}(\sigma B)_{12} =A_{12}^TA_{11}^{-1}A_{12}=A_{22}. \] Hence \(\sigma\cdot B=A\) — contradicting that \(A,B\) lie in different orbits.

7. Step 6 — the construction and separation

Proof of the Theorem. Assume the equal-rank case (Step 2) with representative fixed (Step 3). Apply the Lagrange template ([[lagrange-separation-invariants]]) with:
• positions \(\mathcal P_r\) (only the first \(r\) rows — Lemma W licenses this);
• nodes \(\Lambda=\{A_{kl}\}\cup\{B_{kl}\}\), all entry values of both matrices, \(|\Lambda|\le 2\binom{n+1}2=n^2+n\) — all of them are needed because \((\sigma\cdot G)_{ij}=G_{\sigma^{-1}(i)\sigma^{-1}(j)}\) can be any entry as \(\sigma\) varies;
• indicators \(\ell_{ij}\) of the value \(A_{ij}\) on \(\Lambda\), \(\deg\ell_{ij}\le|\Lambda|-1\le n^2+n-1\);
• \(\displaystyle Q(G)=\sum_{\sigma\in S_n}\ \prod_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P_r} \ell_{ij}\big((\sigma\cdot G)_{ij}\big)\).
Invariance: replacing \(G\) by \(\pi\cdot G\) reindexes \(\sigma\mapsto\sigma\pi\) — a bijection of \(S_n\). The arguments are honest matrix entries, which \(S_n\) permutes: this is where the template's design rule holds (and where Cholesky failed). \(Q(A)\ge1\): every argument \((\sigma A)_{ij}\) is an entry-value of \(A\), hence a node, so each factor is \(0\) or \(1\); the \(\sigma=\mathrm{id}\) term is \(\prod\ell_{ij}(A_{ij})=1\); all terms are \(\ge0\). \(Q(B)=0\): for each \(\sigma\), Lemma W provides \((i,j)\in\mathcal P_r\) with \((\sigma B)_{ij}\neq A_{ij}\); that argument is an entry-value of \(B\), hence a node different in value from \(A_{ij}\), hence a root of \(\ell_{ij}\) — the term vanishes. Degree: \(\deg Q\le|\mathcal P_r|\cdot(n^2+n-1)=N_r(n^2+n-1)\le N_d(n^2+n-1)\). Pull back: \(p(X)=Q(X^TX)\) is \(O(d)\times S_n\)-invariant of degree \(\le 2N_d(n^2+n-1)\) and separates \(X,Y\) (Step 1). Together with the unequal-rank separators of degree \(\le2n\) (Step 2), the set of all invariants of degree \(\le 2N_d(n^2+n-1)\) is separating.
Validated in exact arithmetic. Script validate_first_d_rows.py (integer configurations, rational arithmetic — no floating point): T1 completion identity (60 trials, \(d\in\{2,3\}\), \(n\in\{5,6\}\)); T2 separation \(Q(A)\ge1\), \(Q(B)=0\) on random inequivalent pairs; T3 invariance under random permutations; T4 same-orbit sanity; T5 rank-deficient case (\(r<d\)). 7/7 test groups pass.

8. How each gap of the Cholesky proof is repaired

Gap in the note's proof (finding #5)Repair here
(a) \(S_n\) does not permute Cholesky coordinates Coordinates are the first-\(r\)-rows entries; the \(\sigma\)-sum ranges over the entry permutation action directly — invariance is a reindexing, no induced action needed.
(b) Cholesky coordinates are not polynomial (square roots) \(Q\) is a polynomial in the entries \(G_{ij}\); \(p=Q\circ\Phi\) is a polynomial in \(X\). The completion identity is used only in the witness argument (Lemma W), never to build coordinates.
(c) the trapezoidal chart may miss \(A,B\) Only \(A\) needs an invertible leading block, arranged by a per-pair permutation (Lemma P); \(B\) is arbitrary; ranks below \(d\) descend to \(r=\rank A\); unequal ranks are handled by power sums (Lemma R).

9. What remains open

Bound chain. \[ \underbrace{\tfrac{(n^2-n)(n^2-n+2)}{4}}_{\text{Gram + G\"obel (proved)}}\ \longrightarrow\ \underbrace{2N_d(n^2+n-1)\sim 2dn^3}_{\textbf{this page (proved)}}\ \longrightarrow\ \underbrace{4d^2n^2}_{\text{open}} . \] The residual bottleneck is the node count: each indicator must vanish on all \(\sim n^2\) entry values of \(B\), because a permuted entry can be any entry. Reaching \(O(d^2n^2)\) needs node sets of size \(O(dn)\) — e.g. controlling which entries can appear at first-\(r\)-rows positions, or an interpolation basis adapted to \(\mathcal G_d\). See Audit D2. No lower bound for \(D(n,d)\), \(d<n\), is known (D3).
