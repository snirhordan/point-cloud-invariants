The Theorems

The universal denominator packages Molien; the divisibility theorem — proved here via an equivariant Koszul complex — says invariants with trivial zero set force their degrees to be divisible by the "rotation data" of every group element. The cyclotomic facts turn that into a number.

1. The universal denominator

Definition The universal denominator of \(K[V]^G\) is \[ p(t)=\lcm_{g\in G}\ \det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)\big), \] normalized so \(p(0)=1\).

By Molien, \(H(K[V]^G,t)=\frac1{|G|}\sum_g\frac1{\det(I-t\rho(g))}\); putting the sum over the common denominator \(p(t)\) shows \(q(t):=p(t)\,H(K[V]^G,t)\in K[t]\). So \(p(t)\) is a denominator for the Hilbert series. The theorem below shows it is forced to divide \(\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\) for any invariants cutting out the origin — which is exactly the statement that constrains their degrees \(d_i\).

2. The divisibility theorem

Theorem — Derksen 2005, Thm 1.10(b) Let \(f_1,\dots,f_r\in K[V]^G\) be homogeneous invariants of degrees \(d_1,\dots,d_r\) with common zero set \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\). Then for every \(g\in G\), \[ \det\!\big(I-t\,\rho(g)\big)\ \Big|\ \prod_{i=1}^{r}\big(1-t^{d_i}\big)\quad\text{in }\C[t], \] and therefore \(p(t)\mid \prod_{i=1}^{r}(1-t^{d_i})\).

The tool: the equivariant Koszul complex

Write \(R=K[V]\) (extend scalars to \(\C\)). Attach to \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) the Koszul complex. Let \(E=\C^r\) with basis \(e_1,\dots,e_r\), and give \(e_i\) the internal degree \(d_i\). Put \(K_j=\Lambda^j E\otimes_\C R\), a free \(R\)-module with basis \(e_S=e_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e_{i_j}\) (\(S=\{i_1<\cdots<i_j\}\)); as a graded module, \[ K_j=\bigoplus_{|S|=j} R(-d_S),\qquad d_S=\sum_{i\in S}d_i, \] where \(R(-m)\) is \(R\) shifted up by \(m\) in degree. The differential \(\partial: K_j\to K_{j-1}\), \[ \partial(e_S\otimes a)=\sum_{l=1}^{j}(-1)^{l-1} f_{i_l}\,\big(e_{S\setminus i_l}\otimes a\big), \] lowers exterior degree by one and preserves total (internal) degree, since \(f_{i_l}\) has degree \(d_{i_l}\). It satisfies \(\partial^2=0\).

Equivariance. \(G\) acts on \(R\) as before and trivially on each \(e_i\) — legitimate precisely because the \(f_i\) are invariant, so \(\partial\) commutes with \(G\). Thus \(K_\bullet\) is a complex of graded \(\C G\)-modules.

Lemma — homology is finite-dimensional Each homology module \(H_j(K_\bullet)\) is a finite-dimensional graded \(\C G\)-module.
Proof. The Koszul homology of \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) is annihilated by the ideal \((f_1,\dots,f_r)\), so it is supported on \(V(f_1,\dots,f_r)=\{0\}\). Concretely, localize at any homogeneous prime \(\mathfrak q\neq\mm=R_+\): since \(V(I)=\{0\}\) we have \(I\not\subseteq\mathfrak q\), so some \(f_i\) is a unit in \(R_{\mathfrak q}\); multiplication by a unit is null-homotopic on its Koszul complex, making \((K_\bullet)_{\mathfrak q}\) exact. Hence every \(H_j\) is a finitely generated graded \(R\)-module supported only at \(\mm\), i.e. \(\mm\)-power-torsion, hence finite-dimensional over \(\C\).

The count: graded equivariant Euler characteristic

For a graded \(\C G\)-module \(M=\bigoplus_d M_d\) with finite-dimensional pieces and \(g\in G\), define the graded \(g\)-trace \[ \chi_g(M):=\sum_{d}\tr(g\mid M_d)\,t^d\ \in\ \C[[t]]. \] It is additive on short exact sequences of graded \(G\)-modules, so for the finite complex \(K_\bullet\), the alternating sum over the complex equals the alternating sum over its homology (the graded-equivariant Euler characteristic is a homology invariant): \[ \sum_{j\ge0}(-1)^j\,\chi_g(K_j)\ =\ \sum_{j\ge0}(-1)^j\,\chi_g\!\big(H_j(K_\bullet)\big). \tag{$\ast$}\]

Left side. By Lemma 1 of the Background, \(\chi_g(R)=1/\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})\); write this as \(\Delta_g(t)^{-1}\). A shift \(R(-m)\) multiplies by \(t^m\), and \(g\) acts trivially on each generator \(e_S\), so \(\chi_g(K_j)=\big(\sum_{|S|=j}t^{d_S}\big)\,\Delta_g^{-1}\). Summing with signs and using \(\sum_{j}(-1)^j\!\sum_{|S|=j}t^{d_S}=\prod_{i=1}^r(1-t^{d_i})\), \[ \sum_j(-1)^j\chi_g(K_j)\ =\ \frac{\prod_{i=1}^{r}(1-t^{d_i})}{\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})}. \]

Right side. Each \(H_j\) is finite-dimensional (Lemma), so each \(\chi_g(H_j)\) is a polynomial in \(t\); the alternating sum is therefore a polynomial.

Conclusion of the Theorem. By \((\ast)\), \(\dfrac{\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})}{\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})}\in\C[t]\), i.e. \(\det(I-t\rho(g)^{-1})\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\). As \(g\) ranges over \(G\), so does \(g^{-1}\), giving the same statement with \(\rho(g)\); hence \(\det(I-t\rho(g))\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\) for all \(g\), and taking the lcm over \(g\), \(p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\).

Why this is the honest self-contained proof. The only inputs are the per-element graded trace \(1/\det(I-t\rho(g))\) (Background, Lemma 1), the fact that a trivial zero set makes Koszul homology \(\mm\)-torsion (hence finite-dimensional), and additivity of the Euler characteristic. No black box remains.

3. The separating corollary (Harm's Corollary)

Corollary If \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) are homogeneous separating invariants of degrees \(d_1,\dots,d_r\), then \(p(t)\mid\prod_{i=1}^{r}(1-t^{d_i})\).
Proof. By Lemma 2 (Background), separating invariants have zero set \(\{0\}\); apply the Theorem. (Lemma 3 additionally shows \(K[V]^G\) is module-finite over \(K[f_1,\dots,f_r]\), matching the phrasing in Harm's note, but the Koszul proof already used only the zero-set condition.)

4. Cyclotomic facts

These convert the divisibility \(p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\) into a numerical lower bound on \(\max_i d_i\), once we know \(p(t)\) has a high-degree irreducible factor.

Lemma — cyclotomic toolkit Let \(\Phi_m(t)\) be the \(m\)-th cyclotomic polynomial. Then:
(a) \(t^m-1=\prod_{e\mid m}\Phi_e(t)\), and \(\Phi_m(t)\mid(1-t^{d})\iff m\mid d\);
(b) \(\deg\Phi_m=\varphi(m)\); for distinct primes \(p\neq q\), \(\varphi(pq)=(p-1)(q-1)\);
(c) \(\Phi_m\) is irreducible over \(\Q\) (hence over \(\Z\)).
Proof. (a) Grouping the roots of \(t^m-1\) (all \(m\)-th roots of unity, each simple) by their exact order gives \(t^m-1=\prod_{e\mid m}\Phi_e\). The roots of \(\Phi_m\) are the primitive \(m\)-th roots of unity; such a root satisfies \(\zeta^d=1\) iff \(m\mid d\), and \(\Phi_m\) is squarefree, so \(\Phi_m\mid t^d-1\) iff \(m\mid d\) (up to the sign, \(1-t^d\)). (b) \(\deg\Phi_m\) counts primitive \(m\)-th roots of unity, i.e. \(\varphi(m)\); multiplicativity gives \(\varphi(pq)=(p-1)(q-1)\). (c) Classical (Gauss): \(\Phi_m\) is the minimal polynomial over \(\Q\) of a primitive \(m\)-th root of unity, hence irreducible; being monic with integer coefficients, it is irreducible over \(\Z\).
Assembled engine. If \(p(t)\) has \(1-t^{pq}\) as a factor for distinct primes \(p,q\), then \(\Phi_{pq}\mid p(t)\mid\prod_i(1-t^{d_i})\). Irreducibility forces \(\Phi_{pq}\mid(1-t^{d_i})\) for a single \(i\), so \(pq\mid d_i\) and in particular \(d_i\ge\deg\Phi_{pq}=(p-1)(q-1)\). The Application produces exactly such a factor with \(pq\approx n^2/4\).
